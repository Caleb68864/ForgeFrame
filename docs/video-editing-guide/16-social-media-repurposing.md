# Chapter 16: Social Media and Repurposing

**Part 5 — Output**

A 20-minute tutorial takes a day to shoot, a day to edit, and an afternoon to publish. That investment produces one YouTube video. With an additional hour of work, it can produce three to five short-form clips across YouTube Shorts, Instagram Reels, and TikTok -- each reaching a different audience on a platform that would never show them your full tutorial.

This chapter covers how to identify the best moments in a long tutorial for short-form content, how to edit differently for short-form compared to long-form, and how to prepare platform-specific assets without re-editing the entire video from scratch.

---

## Why Repurpose

There are three practical reasons to invest in repurposing, and they compound over time.

### Reach

YouTube Shorts, Instagram Reels, and TikTok each have algorithm-driven discovery feeds that actively push short-form content to users who have never heard of you. The long-form YouTube algorithm is primarily recommendation-based -- it shows your videos to people who already watch similar content. Short-form feeds use a different distribution model: a clip with strong early engagement gets shown to a progressively larger cold audience regardless of your subscriber count.

A creator with 500 YouTube subscribers can get 50,000 views on a Reel. That same creator will almost never get 50,000 views on a long-form video until their subscriber count is significantly higher. Short-form platforms offer the fastest path to audience discovery for new channels.

### Discoverability

Each clip is an independent entry point to your work. A viewer who discovers you through a 45-second clip showing a specific Kdenlive trick may not watch your 25-minute tutorial on the same topic -- but they might follow you. When you publish that next tutorial, they are already there.

Think of clips as trailers. They demonstrate your teaching style, your depth of knowledge, and whether your communication approach matches what the viewer needs -- all in under 60 seconds.

### Algorithm Feeds

Social platform algorithms are hungry. Consistent posting schedules -- even two or three Shorts a week -- train the algorithm to show your content regularly. Posting one long-form video a week with no short-form content gives the algorithm one data point. The same week's worth of clips gives it five. More data points means more frequent distribution tests, which means faster audience growth.

## How Short-Form Is Different From Long-Form

Long-form and short-form video editing have opposite structures. Understanding the difference before you cut a single clip will save you hours of re-edits.

### Long-Form Structure

A tutorial is a journey. You introduce the problem, demonstrate the setup, walk through each step, explain why decisions are made, show the result, and recap. The viewer commits to watching because they have a specific learning goal. You can spend 45 seconds explaining why white balance matters before you show how to fix it. The viewer will wait.

### Short-Form Structure

A short-form clip is a punch. The viewer is scrolling. They will give you 1-2 seconds to prove the clip is worth stopping for. If those seconds do not deliver an immediately interesting hook, they scroll past -- and the algorithm registers a skip, which suppresses future distribution.

This inverts the long-form tutorial structure almost entirely:

| Long-form | Short-form |
|-----------|------------|
| Problem → Context → Solution | Solution (or best moment) → Context (brief) → Payoff |
| Build-up is expected and valued | Build-up causes scroll-past |
| 30-second intro is acceptable | 30-second intro is fatal |
| Chapters let viewers skip context | Every second must earn its place |
| Explanation-first teaching | Show-first, explain-second teaching |

The hook -- the opening 1-2 seconds -- determines whether the clip gets watched. Not the content, not the production quality, not even how useful the information is. If the hook does not create immediate attention, none of the rest matters.

---

## The Hook-First Principle

Every short-form clip needs a hook in the first 1-2 seconds. A hook is not an introduction. It is the most interesting or surprising moment in the clip, placed first.

### What Makes a Good Hook

**A visual action already happening.** Cut into the middle of a demonstration, not the beginning. The viewer sees something interesting and wants to know what is happening.

**A bold declarative statement.** "Most editors get white balance wrong." "This export setting is destroying your video quality." "There is a faster way to do this." Bold statements create a pattern interrupt -- the viewer has to hear the resolution.

**A question with an implied answer that is surprising.** "What if you never had to set white balance manually?" The viewer wants to know the answer.

**A striking visual contrast.** Before/after comparisons shown immediately -- even a split-second cut between bad and good -- create curiosity about how the transformation happened.

### What Kills a Hook

**Introducing yourself.** "Hey, I'm [name] and in this video..." is a hook killer. The viewer does not care who you are in the first second. They care whether the clip is worth their time.

**Restating what the title/thumbnail already said.** If the thumbnail says "Kdenlive Color Grading Trick," do not open with "Today I'm going to show you a Kdenlive color grading trick." You have just repeated the promise without advancing it.

**Slow motion or fade-in openings.** Every frame of lead-in is a frame where the viewer might scroll away.

**Requiring context from the full video.** "So as I mentioned in the previous section..." is an automatic scroll-past trigger. Each clip must be fully self-contained.

## What Makes a Good Clip

Not every moment in a long tutorial is a clip candidate. The best short-form clips share four characteristics:

### Self-Contained

The clip must make sense without having watched anything before or after it. A viewer who has never seen your channel should be able to watch 45 seconds and understand what happened and why it matters.

This is often the hardest constraint to meet in tutorial content because tutorials are deliberately sequential. Good clip candidates are moments where a technique is demonstrated in isolation -- a single fix, a single adjustment, a single insight.

### Strong Visual

Short-form content is consumed on mobile, mostly without sound. The visual must be interesting on its own. A clip of someone clicking through menus with no visible result is not compelling even with good narration. A clip that shows a dramatic before/after transformation, a satisfying finishing motion, or an unexpected visual trick has a visual hook that works with or without audio.

### Clear Takeaway

The viewer should be able to articulate what they learned in one sentence. "I learned that you can fix choppy Kdenlive playback by enabling proxy clips." That is a clear takeaway. "I watched someone edit a video" is not.

### Appropriate Length

Platform attention windows differ:

| Platform | Ideal clip length | Max |
|----------|-------------------|-----|
| YouTube Shorts | 30-50 seconds | 60 seconds |
| Instagram Reels | 15-30 seconds | 90 seconds |
| TikTok | 15-30 seconds | 10 minutes (but short wins) |

These are guidelines, not rules. A 55-second clip that earns every second outperforms a 25-second clip that meanders. But as a starting point, aim for the 30-50 second range for maximum cross-platform compatibility.

## Platform Aspect Ratios

Each platform has a preferred format, and uploading the wrong aspect ratio results in black bars, cropping, or visual degradation.

### 9:16 Vertical (Shorts, Reels, TikTok)

9:16 is the native format for all major short-form platforms. This is the full-screen vertical format designed for mobile viewing. If you shoot your tutorials in the standard 16:9 landscape format, you have two options for creating vertical clips:

**Option 1: Reframe for vertical.** In Kdenlive, create a new 9:16 project and import the landscape footage. Use the position and zoom effect to reframe the content into the vertical frame. This works well for close-up demonstrations where the key action fits in a vertical crop (a hand adjusting a slider, a UI panel, a face shot).

**Option 2: Use a split-screen or letterbox style.** Place the landscape footage in the center of the vertical frame, with custom graphics, captions, or a blurred background fill above and below. This keeps the full 16:9 frame visible while fitting the vertical container. It is a common format on TikTok and Reels for screen recordings.

For screen recordings and desktop content (Kdenlive interface, terminal, timeline), the reframe approach is difficult because UI panels are horizontal. The split-screen approach with a blurred version of the interface as the background works well for this content type.

### 1:1 Square (Instagram Feed, LinkedIn)

Square format is less common for tutorial content but still used for Instagram feed posts and LinkedIn video posts. It is a reasonable compromise format -- it fills more of the mobile screen than 16:9 landscape without requiring a full vertical reframe.

In Kdenlive, a 1:1 square export profile can be created by setting the output resolution to 1080 × 1080 and using the same reframe-or-letterbox approach as vertical.

### 16:9 Landscape (YouTube Standard, Twitter/X)

Standard landscape is appropriate for Twitter/X video posts and for YouTube when you are not targeting Shorts specifically. If you publish the full tutorial, clips shared as landscape work on platforms where 16:9 is native.

For the same clip published to multiple platforms, the production order is:

1. Edit the clip in its native aspect ratio (usually matching the original footage)
2. Export for Shorts/Reels/TikTok in 9:16
3. Export for standard YouTube and Twitter in 16:9
4. Export for Instagram feed in 1:1 if needed

Do not try to find a single crop that works for all three. You will compromise every platform. The right approach is platform-specific exports from the same clip edit.

## Finding Clip Candidates in a Long Tutorial

You do not need to watch the entire tutorial to find clip candidates. Scan for these structural patterns:

**Standalone technique demonstrations.** Any time you show how to do one specific thing -- adjust a value, apply a filter, configure a setting -- without relying on earlier context, that is a clip candidate.

**Before/after moments.** Any time you show a comparison between a worse version and a better version, you have a visual hook built in.

**Common mistake corrections.** "Most people do X, but actually you should do Y because Z." This structure is a natural short-form format: hook (the common mistake), flip (the correction), payoff (the reason).

**Named techniques or shortcuts.** A specific keyboard shortcut, a named workflow technique, or a counterintuitive setting that produces a good result -- these are shareable and memorable at clip length.

**Moments that surprised you while editing.** If you were editing and thought "oh, that's interesting" -- that reaction is a signal. If it surprised an experienced editor, it will be new information to most viewers.

> **ForgeFrame:** Use `/ff-social-clips` to scan the transcript automatically for clip candidates. The skill scores each candidate on hook strength, clarity, and engagement.
>
> ```
> social_find_clips(workspace_path="projects/my-tutorial", max_clips=5)
> ```
>
> Results are ranked by `overall_score`. Any clip above 0.6 is a strong candidate. Review the top three, watch those segments in the timeline, and confirm they work as standalone clips before exporting.

## Generating the Social Package

Once you have identified which clips you want to export, you need platform-specific assets for each: a title, caption, overlay text suggestions, and post copy.

> **ForgeFrame:** `ff-social-clips` generates the complete package in one step:
>
> ```
> social_generate_package(workspace_path="projects/my-tutorial", max_clips=5, aspect_ratio="9:16")
> ```
>
> This writes to `reports/social/`:
> - `clips_manifest.json` -- export specs for all clips (in/out points, target platform, aspect ratio)
> - `clip_1_post.txt`, `clip_2_post.txt`, etc. -- platform-specific post copy
> - `social_summary.md` -- overview of all clips with timestamps, scores, and recommendations
>
> For platform-specific post text:
>
> ```
> social_clip_post(workspace_path="projects/my-tutorial", clip_index=0, platform="instagram")
> ```
>
> Supported platforms: `youtube`, `instagram`, `tiktok`, `twitter`
>
> **Manually:** For each clip, write: (1) a title under 50 characters, (2) a two-sentence caption explaining what the viewer will learn, (3) three to five hashtags, (4) platform-specific adjustments (TikTok needs a shorter hook; Instagram can use longer captions). Write these in a single note before you start exporting -- batch the writing, then batch the exporting.

## Exporting Clips from Kdenlive

After identifying your clip candidates and their in/out points, here is the Kdenlive workflow for exporting vertical or square clips:

### Approach 1: Set In/Out Points on the Timeline

1. In your existing project, mark the In point (I key) and Out point (O key) around the clip segment.
2. In the Render dialog, check **"Render selected zone only"**.
3. Adjust the resolution to match the target platform (1080 × 1920 for 9:16 vertical, 1080 × 1080 for square).
4. Add position/zoom effects to reframe the content before rendering if needed.

### Approach 2: Duplicate the Project for Each Clip

For complex reframes or clips that need significant editing:

1. Duplicate the project file (`File → Save As...`)
2. Delete everything outside the clip window
3. Add a 9:16 or 1:1 sequence profile
4. Reframe and edit the clip independently
5. Export normally

Approach 1 is faster for simple clips with minimal reframing. Approach 2 gives you full control if the clip needs its own edit with added captions, text overlays, or transitions.

## Platform-Specific Publishing Notes

### YouTube Shorts

- Shorts are displayed in a dedicated feed and are not mixed with standard long-form results
- Add `#Shorts` in the title or description -- YouTube uses this as a distribution signal
- Shorts do not support chapters or chapters timestamps
- Shorts can be linked to your long-form video using cards or end screens in the long-form video

### Instagram Reels

- Instagram penalizes content with visible TikTok watermarks -- export a clean version for Instagram
- Captions are essential on Instagram; many viewers watch without sound
- Instagram's algorithm currently favors Reels over static feed posts for discovery

### TikTok

- TikTok's algorithm is highly responsive to engagement in the first hour after posting
- Post at times when your target audience is active (early morning, lunch, evening)
- TikTok's native caption feature is separate from the video itself -- use it for additional context
- TikTok compresses video more aggressively than other platforms; upload at the highest quality available to preserve detail after compression

### Twitter/X

- Twitter/X video plays inline and autoplay without sound
- Short captions that add context beyond the title perform better than captions that just repeat the title
- Twitter video analytics report completion rates, which help evaluate whether the hook is working

## Building a Repurposing Workflow

Ad hoc repurposing -- exporting a clip when you feel like it -- rarely becomes a sustainable habit. The way to make repurposing consistent is to build it into the publishing workflow as a standard step, not an optional extra.

A minimal weekly workflow:

1. **While editing:** Mark potential clip moments with a timeline marker labeled `CLIP:` followed by a short note. Do not stop to evaluate them in depth -- just mark them so you do not have to re-watch everything later.

2. **After export:** Review your `CLIP:` markers. Pick two to three. Evaluate each for hook strength, self-containment, and clear takeaway. Cut that list to one or two strong candidates.

3. **After YouTube publish:** Export clips, generate post assets, and schedule them across platforms. This takes 30-60 minutes when you have the clips already identified.

4. **Stagger publishing:** Do not publish clips the same day as the long-form tutorial. Clips work best when they drive traffic back to a video that already has some watch time. Publishing clips two to five days after the main video gives the main video time to start accumulating views before the clip audience arrives.

> **ForgeFrame:** The `CLIP:` marker naming convention integrates directly with `ff-social-clips`. The skill preferentially evaluates segments near markers with the `CLIP:` prefix when scanning for candidates.

## Quality and Authenticity

Short-form content for tutorial channels does not need to look like a professional ad. In fact, over-polished clips often underperform compared to clips that feel like genuine moments from real work.

A clip of you solving a real problem you encountered while editing -- with visible hesitation, a correction, and an honest "here is why this was confusing" -- tends to perform better than a perfectly scripted 45-second tutorial because it is authentic. The viewer recognizes the experience of being stuck and feeling unstuck.

The production constraint for clips is the same as for long-form tutorials: adequate audio (see Chapter 10), clear visuals, and no technical distractions. Beyond that, the quality that matters is whether the content is genuinely useful and genuinely you.
