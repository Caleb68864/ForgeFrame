# Chapter 05: Filming Your Tutorial

**Part II — Production**

---

Good editing can save a mediocre recording. Great editing cannot save a bad one. The good news: bad recordings come from a handful of fixable mistakes, and fixing them does not require expensive gear. This chapter gives you everything you need to shoot usable footage with a phone or entry-level DSLR and roughly $100-150 in equipment.

The goal here is not a Netflix production. The goal is footage that looks clean, sounds clear, and cuts together without surprises in the edit bay.

---

## Camera Settings

Before you press record, lock in your camera settings. These four settings control the look of your footage more than any other factor. Set them once per session, write them down, and keep them consistent across all your shots.

### Resolution: Shoot 4K, Deliver 1080p

If your camera supports 4K, use it -- even if your final video will be 1080p.

The reason is reframing headroom. When you shoot 4K and edit in a 1080p timeline, you have roughly four times the pixel area to work with. That means you can push in on a shot in post without losing quality, reframe a slightly-off composition, or stabilize shaky footage without cropping into mush.

In practice: if you set up a talking-head shot and your eye line is a little low, or you realize in the edit that the composition feels cramped, 4K gives you the room to fix it digitally. With 1080p source footage, your options are limited.

If your phone or camera only shoots 1080p, that is fine. Billions of great tutorial videos exist in 1080p. Just be aware that what you see in the viewfinder is what you get.

### Frame Rate: 30fps for Most Tutorial Work

Set your camera to 30 frames per second (fps) and leave it there.

- **30fps** is the standard for online tutorial content. It looks natural on-screen and is compatible with every editing platform and delivery format.
- **24fps** is the cinematic look used in film and narrative content. Technically fine for YouTube, but feels slightly "movie-ish" for tutorials. Use it only if you have a strong aesthetic reason.
- **60fps** is for slow-motion work only. If you are showing a rapid hand motion and want to slow it down in post, shoot that clip at 60fps. Do not shoot your entire tutorial at 60fps -- it looks unnatural at normal speed, and the files are twice as large.

Pick one frame rate per session and stick to it. Mixing frame rates in the same project creates headaches when they need to play back together.

### Shutter Speed: The 180-Degree Rule

Your shutter speed should be roughly double your frame rate. This is called the 180-degree rule, borrowed from film cinematography, and it controls how motion looks in your footage.

At 30fps: **set shutter to 1/60 second.**  
At 60fps (slow-mo clips): **set shutter to 1/120 second.**

Why does this matter? If your shutter is too fast (say, 1/500 at 30fps), motion looks choppy and stroboscopic -- unnatural to the eye. If it is too slow, motion blurs excessively. The doubled-shutter gives a small, natural motion blur on each frame that matches how human vision perceives movement.

**The catch:** indoors, 1/60 may not let in enough light. You have two options:

1. Add more light (ideal -- see the Lighting section below)
2. Raise ISO to compensate (adds grain/noise -- acceptable in small amounts)

Do not raise shutter speed to compensate for dim light. You will pay for it in choppy-looking motion.

### White Balance: Why Auto Will Burn You

Auto white balance (AWB) is convenient and dangerous. Leave it on auto and your camera will continuously adjust color temperature as you move, change framing, or if a cloud passes outside. Cuts that should match will have different color casts. Your hands may look orange in one shot and blue in the next.

**Set white balance manually.** Use these starting points:

| Light source | Color temperature |
|---|---|
| Daylight / overcast window | 5600K |
| LED panels (most consumer units) | 5600K |
| Tungsten / incandescent bulbs | 3200K |
| Fluorescent office lights | 4000K |

For best accuracy, use a **gray card** (a neutral 18% gray card, available for $5-10). Hold it in frame under your actual lighting, point your camera at it, and use your camera's custom white balance function to sample it. This eliminates the guesswork and gives you a consistent baseline.

Write down your white balance setting at the start of every session. If you film across multiple days, matching shots becomes much easier when you recorded the setting the first day.

## Lighting

Lighting is the single highest-leverage improvement you can make to footage quality. A phone shot in good light beats a DSLR shot in bad light, every time.

The core principle is control: you want the light to come from where *you* decided it should come from, at the intensity you chose, and to stay there throughout your recording. Natural light from windows is beautiful but inconsistent -- a cloud can blow your exposure mid-sentence. The goal of a basic lighting setup is to make the light reproducible and independent of the weather outside.

### Setup 1: Talking Head (On-Camera Presenter)

When you are on screen -- facing the camera, explaining or demonstrating verbally -- use a two-light setup:

**Key light:** Your primary light source. Position it at roughly 45 degrees to one side of your face, slightly above eye level (think: 2 o'clock position if your face is the clock). This gives shape and dimension to your face.

**Fill light (or bounce board):** On the opposite side, at a lower intensity. The fill softens the shadow the key creates. You can use a second LED panel at lower power, or a white foam board (see Budget Gear below) to bounce light from the key back onto the shadow side.

The goal is a ratio where one side of your face is well-lit and the other side is lit about half as much -- not fully dark (dramatic) and not equally lit (flat). Both extremes look odd for tutorial content.

**What to avoid:** Never put a bright window behind you. It makes you a silhouette. Either sit with your back to the wall and the window in front of you (window as your key light) or block the window and use LEDs.

### Setup 2: Overhead Workbench (Hands-On Demonstrations)

When you are showing work on a table -- soldering, woodworking, drawing, cooking, typing -- you need to light the work surface, not your face.

**Use two matched LED panels**, one on each side of the work surface, angled down at 45 degrees. Matched brightness, matched color temperature. This symmetric setup eliminates the hand shadow problem: with a single overhead light, every hand motion creates a shadow on exactly the thing you are trying to show. Two equal sources from both sides cancel each other out.

Mount the panels on small desktop stands or clamp them to shelving above the bench. The key is getting them off the camera axis so the light comes from the sides, not straight down.

**Height matters:** Too high and the light grazes the surface (emphasizes texture, good for woodgrain, bad for text). Too low and you get glare. Start at 45 degrees and adjust by watching the monitor.

You can also use this setup for keyboard close-ups, circuit boards, or any tabletop detail work.

### Setup 3: Detail Closeups

When you are shooting tight -- a single component, a weld, a screen detail, a small tool -- you have two distinct lighting choices depending on what you want to emphasize:

**On-axis (flat, even):** Position a single LED directly above or behind the camera, pointing at the subject. This gives even, shadow-free illumination. Good for showing color accurately and for readable text.

**Raking (side-lit, texture):** Position a single LED to the extreme side of the subject, nearly parallel to the surface. This rakes across the surface at a shallow angle and creates strong shadows in any texture or relief. Excellent for showing surface detail, engraving, texture, or mechanical parts where the 3D structure matters.

Neither is universally better. Ask yourself: do I want to show what it looks like (on-axis) or show what it feels like (raking)?

### Budget Lighting Gear

You do not need expensive equipment to implement the setups above.

**Neewer 660 LED panel (~$40 each):** The standard starting point for budget tutorial lighting. Adjustable brightness, adjustable color temperature, runs on AC or batteries. Two of them cover talking head and workbench setups. Not the highest quality light, but consistent and controllable.

**White foam board (~$5):** Available at any craft or dollar store. A 20"×30" sheet propped opposite your key light acts as a fill reflector that costs essentially nothing. It is less controllable than a second light but works well for talking head setups where the fill just needs to soften shadows.

**Window light:** Usable as a key light if the window is to your side (not behind you) and the weather is consistent. The problem is it changes -- as clouds move, your exposure and color temperature shift. If you film near a window, supplement with an LED fill on the shadow side to add stability. If you cannot control the window light, black out the window and go all-LED.

**Total for a basic two-light setup:** Two Neewer 660s + foam board + a pair of cheap desktop stands = roughly $100.

## Audio Capture

Your audience will forgive video problems they would never forgive audio problems. Choppy video, slightly soft focus, imperfect exposure -- viewers tolerate all of it. Hollow audio, background hum, or inconsistent levels will drive them away.

### Choosing the Right Microphone for the Job

Different recording scenarios call for different microphone types:

**Lapel (lavalier) mic -- for on-camera talking:** A small clip-on mic that attaches to your shirt near your chest. Because it stays close to your mouth regardless of where you look, it gives consistent audio even when you turn your head. Best choice for presenter-style shots where you are speaking directly to camera. The Rode Wireless Go II (~$150) is a common wireless lav setup; for a budget option, a wired lav like the Rode SmartLav+ (~$60) plugged into your phone or a recorder works just as well.

**Shotgun mic -- for workshop and ambient capture:** Mounts on top of the camera and picks up sound in a narrow forward-facing cone. Good for ambient recording of your work area -- tool sounds, keyboard sounds, background sound design. The Rode VideoMicro (~$60) is a compact, camera-mountable option. Note: a shotgun mic on-camera for talking head is workable but not ideal -- it picks up room reflections because the source (your mouth) is further away than a lapel.

**USB condenser mic -- for voiceover recorded in post:** If you plan to narrate over your footage after the fact (which is often easier than narrating live), a USB condenser mic gives you clean studio-quality audio recorded at your desk. The Audio-Technica AT2020 USB (~$100) is the reliable standard. Record voiceover in a treated space -- see Chapter 10 for room treatment basics.

You can also do this manually: place any decent directional mic 6-12 inches from your mouth, shield it from room reflections with foam or blankets, and record directly to a phone or portable recorder.

### Gain Staging: The Numbers That Matter

Set your microphone gain so that **peaks land at -12dB**. This is not an aesthetic preference -- it is the number that gives you clean audio with room to work in post.

The rule: **peaks at -12dB, never exceed -6dB.**

Why? Digital audio that exceeds 0dB clips and distorts -- unrecoverable, no fix in post. Leaving 6-12dB of headroom protects you from unexpected loud sounds (a hand slap, a tool drop, a cleared throat) that would otherwise clip.

Before every recording session, do a test:

1. Set up as you will for the actual shoot
2. Record 30 seconds while doing the loudest thing you will do in the session (speak at your loudest, make the loudest work noise)
3. Play it back and read the peak meter
4. Adjust gain until peaks land at -12dB

Do this every session. Gain levels drift, and it takes two minutes to verify.

## Sync Strategy: Keeping Audio and Video Aligned

If you are recording audio on a separate device from your camera (a common setup with a dedicated recorder or phone mic), you need a way to sync them in the edit.

### The Clap Method

At the beginning of every take, before you start the actual content:

1. Hold your hands in frame
2. Clap once, sharply
3. Wait a beat, then begin

In the edit, you align the audio spike from the clap on your separate audio track with the visual frame where your hands come together. This gives you a sync point accurate to one frame.

Do this at the start of every take, not just every session. If you stop and restart recording, you need a new sync point.

### Timecode (For Later)

If you eventually move to a multi-camera or multi-device setup, dedicated timecode devices (like the Tentacle Sync) can automate alignment. They broadcast a timecode signal that multiple devices record simultaneously, and editing software aligns them automatically. For single-camera tutorial work, the clap method is more than sufficient.

## VFR Prevention: The Silent Editor Killer

Variable Frame Rate (VFR) is one of the most common causes of audio-sync drift, rendering artifacts, and mysterious timeline problems that seem unfixable. The cause: your camera is not recording at a steady frame rate.

### Why VFR Happens

Most smartphones default to VFR when shooting video. The camera dynamically adjusts frame rate based on scene brightness -- in low light it might drop to 24fps, in bright light it runs at 30fps. This is a feature designed to reduce motion blur in difficult conditions. For editing, it is a problem: your editing software assumes a constant frame rate, so when the camera delivers variable frames, audio gradually drifts out of sync.

You will not notice VFR during recording. You will notice it 40 minutes into an edit when you realize your audio is 1.3 seconds behind your video and there is no easy fix.

### How to Force CFR (Constant Frame Rate)

**iPhone:** The native Camera app defaults to VFR. Use **Filmic Pro** ($15) and enable "Constant Frame Rate" in the format settings. Also enables log shooting and manual exposure if you want them.

**Android:** The native camera varies by manufacturer. Use **Open Camera** (free) and set a fixed frame rate in the video settings.

**GoPro and action cameras:** Check the mode settings. GoPros offer CFR options in specific modes but default to auto in others. Read the manual for your model.

**DSLR and mirrorless cameras:** Most dedicated cameras record CFR by default. Verify in the video menu.

### Verifying With MediaInfo

Before you import any footage into your edit, verify it is CFR using **MediaInfo** (free, available for Windows/Mac/Linux):

1. Open MediaInfo
2. Drag your video file onto it
3. Look for "Frame rate mode" in the Video track
4. It should say **"Constant"** -- if it says **"Variable,"** transcode before editing

You can also do this manually: most video players (VLC, ffprobe) will report frame rate mode on file inspection. Make this a pre-import habit. Five seconds of checking beats two hours of mysterious sync issues.

## Minimum Viable Setup

If you are starting from scratch, here is the priority order for spending your first $100-120. Each item eliminates the biggest remaining quality problem.

| Priority | Item | Cost | What it fixes |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Tripod | ~$25 | Shaky, unusable footage -- the #1 quality killer |
| 2 | Wired lav mic | $30-60 | Hollow, distant-sounding audio |
| 3 | One LED panel | ~$40 | Inconsistent, unflattering light |
| 4 | White foam board | ~$5 | Hard shadows on the dark side of your face |

**Use your existing phone or camera.** Spend on stability, audio, and light first. Camera upgrades come last. A Pixel 6 on a tripod with a lav mic and one LED will outperform a Sony A7 handheld in bad light with the built-in mic, every time.

**Upgrade order:** stability → audio → lighting → camera last.

Once you have this baseline, improvements compound. A second LED panel adds the workbench setup. A wireless lav adds mobility. A USB mic opens up clean voiceover recording. Each upgrade solves a specific problem rather than chasing vague "quality."

>
> You can also do this manually: write out your shots grouped by setup (talking head, overhead, detail), and run through all shots in one setup before changing your lighting or mic position.

## Common Filming Mistakes

> **Sidebar: Mistakes That Kill Good Footage**
>
> **Bright window behind you.** The camera exposes for the bright background and turns you into a silhouette. Always put windows to your side or in front, never behind.
>
> **Cluttered background.** Viewers' eyes drift to anything interesting behind you -- tools, labels, papers. Clear the background or use a physical backdrop. What you see in the viewfinder is distracting; what you don't notice is what viewers will notice.
>
> **No tripod.** Handheld camera for talking head looks amateur and makes the viewer tired. If the shot doesn't need motion, lock it down.
>
> **Not marking tripod position.** If you remove the tripod between sessions, your shots won't match. Put tape on the floor at the tripod feet. Write down your zoom level. Matching shots across sessions requires documented setups.
>
> **Narrating while working.** It sounds natural, but it is almost impossible to do well. Your voice changes when you are concentrating on a task, your mouth turns away from the mic, and you say "um" constantly. Recording voiceover in post -- narrating to a finished rough cut -- gives you clean audio, better pacing, and the ability to re-record a single sentence without reshooting.
>
> **Forgetting the audio check.** "I'll just see how it sounds" is how you end up with a 45-minute session at -30dB (inaudible) or clipped at 0dB (distorted). Two minutes of test recording before every session. Every session.
>
> **Inconsistent white balance.** Leaving AWB on means shots that should cut together look like different times of day. Set it manually and write it down.
